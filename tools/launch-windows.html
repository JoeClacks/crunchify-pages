How It Works

A Hohmann transfer orbit is the most fuel-efficient path between two planets. It is an elliptical orbit whose perihelion (closest point to the Sun) is at Earth's orbit and whose aphelion (farthest point) is at Mars's orbit. A spacecraft follows this half-ellipse for approximately 259 days before arriving at Mars.

For this trajectory to work, Mars must already be at the arrival point when the spacecraft gets there — which only happens at specific times. These opportunities are called launch windows and recur roughly every 26 months (the synodic period: how long until Earth and Mars return to the same relative positions).

This calculator uses simplified Keplerian orbital elements from the NASA/JPL J2000 model to compute planetary positions. The windows shown are Hohmann-optimal dates; actual mission launch windows are determined by C3 (launch energy) budgets and may shift by several weeks. Real missions to Mars — including Curiosity (2011), InSight (2018), and Perseverance (2020) — all launched during windows computed with this method.
